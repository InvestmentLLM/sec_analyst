# Economics Psychology & Market Behavior

## Market Cycle Psychology (Stages)
1. Disbelief — prices recover but investors stay skeptical after a downturn
2. Hope — early buyers see gains, cautious optimism builds
3. Optimism — growth narrative widely accepted, new buyers enter
4. Belief — strong conviction, momentum traders pile in
5. Thrill — everyone claims genius, media coverage peaks, retail FOMO
6. Euphoria — peak valuation, maximum financial risk, bad companies get funded
7. Complacency — small decline, "healthy correction" narrative
8. Anxiety — concern grows, denial phase among longs
9. Denial — "it will come back, it always does"
10. Panic — maximum financial opportunity for disciplined buyers, forced selling
11. Capitulation — giving up, selling at losses regardless of fundamentals
12. Depression — prolonged pessimism, lowest valuations, best long-term entry

## Key Behavioral Biases to Counter in Analysis
- Anchoring Bias: Do not anchor to 52-week highs or historical ATH when evaluating current valuation
- Confirmation Bias: Actively seek bearish data even when bullish on a company
- Recency Bias: One or two great quarters does not define a business; weight multi-year trends
- Loss Aversion: Fear of loss is 2x stronger than pleasure of gain — leads to holding losers too long
- Herding: Most popular stocks are often most dangerous; consensus is frequently late

## Earnings & Guidance Psychology
- Beat and Raise is most bullish catalyst: beats estimates AND raises future guidance
- Miss and Lower is most bearish: misses AND cuts guidance (avoid these companies)
- Guidance cut is often more serious than one earnings miss
- Positive earnings surprise creates momentum for 3-6 months on average
- Revenue miss is typically worse than earnings miss — revenue is harder to manage

## Macro Economic Factors in Company Analysis
- Rising interest rates compress growth stock valuations (higher discount rate on future cash flows)
- High inflation benefits pricing-power companies (luxury, commodities) but hurts thin-margin retailers
- Strong USD hurts multinationals (revenue earned in foreign currencies converts to fewer dollars)
- Recession environment: defensive sectors (utilities, healthcare, consumer staples) outperform
- Early cycle recovery: cyclicals (financials, industrials, consumer discretionary) outperform

## What Management Language Signals in SEC Filings
- "Challenging environment" or "headwinds" = problems ahead, read carefully
- Excessive use of "adjusted," "non-GAAP," or "one-time" items = possible earnings management
- Shift from specific guidance to "we do not provide guidance" = loss of visibility
- Multiple accounting restatements = serious red flag, potential fraud
- Long risk factor sections with vague language = management covering itself legally
- Specific, confident guidance with historical accuracy = trustworthy management team
- Insider buying at open market prices = strong bullish signal
- Executive departures in rapid succession = something wrong internally

## Risk Assessment Framework
- Systematic Risk (market-wide): recession, rising rates, geopolitical events, currency moves
- Unsystematic Risk (company-specific): management turnover, product failure, regulatory action
- Concentration Risk: single customer >20% of revenue is dangerous; single product = vulnerability
- Competitive Moat Assessment: pricing power, switching costs, network effects, cost advantages, intangibles
- Regulatory Risk: antitrust exposure (big tech), FDA approval dependence (biotech), environmental liability

## Mean Reversion Principle
- Companies with above-average margins eventually face competition that compresses them
- Companies with below-average margins in good industries often recover (mean revert upward)
- Revenue growth rates mean-revert to GDP growth over long periods for most businesses
- Exception: businesses with durable competitive moats can sustain above-average returns for decades
