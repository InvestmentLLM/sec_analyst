# Stock Review Methodology & Writing Standards

## How to Structure a Strong Stock Review

### Section Order (follow this sequence in every justification)
1. **Revenue trajectory** — state every year's figure, YoY %, and the 3-yr CAGR. Characterize the trend: accelerating, decelerating, inflecting, stalling.
2. **Margin analysis** — gross, operating, and net margin for every year. Is the business getting more or less profitable as it scales? Expanding margins = operating leverage. Contracting = cost pressure, pricing erosion, or mix shift.
3. **Balance sheet posture** — net cash/debt position, D/E trend, current ratio. Flag if the company is taking on debt faster than it's growing earnings.
4. **Cash generation quality** — FCF trend with FCF margins. Compare OCF to Net Income. A company printing earnings but not generating cash is a yellow flag.
5. **Management credibility check** — does MD&A language match the numbers? Was prior guidance accurate? Quantify the gap between management's stated outlook and actual results.
6. **Key risks with data** — name each risk, cite the specific metric that supports it, and give the year range. Vague risks ("competition could increase") are worthless — quantify them.
7. **What would change the rating** — state the specific conditions under which this becomes a Buy (if currently Hold/Sell) or a Sell (if currently Buy). This forces intellectual honesty.

### Writing Quality Rules
- Never use filler phrases: "overall strong performance," "the company is well-positioned," "continued to execute."
- Every positive claim must have a specific number attached: not "margins improved" but "gross margin expanded from 38.2% in FY2022 to 44.1% in FY2024."
- Every risk must be concrete: not "debt is high" but "D/E ratio of 2.3x as of FY2024, up from 0.8x in FY2021, while revenue grew only 12% over the same period."
- If a company is improving, name the year it turned. If deteriorating, name the year it peaked.
- Avoid false balance — if the data is clearly negative, say so. Do not soften a Sell with three paragraphs of positives.
- Use precise language for conviction: "The data strongly suggests..." vs. "There are early indications..." vs. "It is unclear whether..."

### Rating Language Calibration
- **Strong Buy (85-100):** "Compounding revenue and margin growth over 4+ years, FCF consistently exceeding net income, fortress balance sheet. The risk/reward is exceptional."
- **Buy (70-84):** "Solid multi-year track record with identifiable concerns. Growth is real but decelerating. Appropriate for patient investors."
- **Hold (50-69):** "Mixed signals. One or more key metrics deteriorating. Better entry points likely. Wait for evidence the trend has stabilized."
- **Sell (30-49):** "Structural decline evident across multiple metrics. Near-term catalysts insufficient to reverse the trend. Capital at risk."
- **Strong Sell (0-29):** "Multiple years of deterioration across revenue, margins, and FCF. Balance sheet under stress. Existential risks are non-trivial."

### Bull Case / Bear Case Framework
Always articulate both sides explicitly:
- **Bull case:** What specific data point or catalyst would accelerate the positive trend? (e.g., "gross margin expansion continues to 50%+ as software mix increases — at that margin profile, this would trade at 25x EV/EBITDA")
- **Bear case:** What specific deterioration would invalidate the investment thesis? (e.g., "if revenue growth decelerates below 5% YoY while D/E remains above 1.5x, the valuation premium is unjustifiable")
- **Base case:** What does the multi-year trend suggest is the most likely path?

### Catalyst Identification (include in outlook section)
Positive catalysts to look for:
- Beat-and-raise earnings cycle (company consistently beating consensus)
- Margin inflection: gross margin crossing a key threshold (e.g., 40%, 50%, 60%)
- Debt paydown reducing interest burden
- New product cycle or geographic expansion with early revenue contribution
- Insider buying at open-market prices (officers buying, not options exercising)
- Activist investor involvement with specific operational demands
- Sector tailwind from regulatory change or macro environment shift

Negative catalysts to flag:
- Guidance cut, especially a second consecutive cut
- Customer concentration risk materializing (key customer loss)
- Margin compression as a pricing strategy (discounting to maintain growth)
- Management turnover: CFO or CEO departure before a major product cycle
- Large acquisition at peak valuation (destroys capital)
- Share dilution accelerating faster than revenue growth
- Short interest above 15% with legitimate fundamental basis

## Advanced Fundamental Analysis

### Competitive Moat Assessment (must address in every review)
Rate the moat strength: **None / Narrow / Wide**

- **Cost Advantage:** Is the company the lowest-cost producer in its industry? (airlines: CASM; retailers: COGS/Revenue; manufacturers: gross margin vs peers)
- **Switching Costs:** How painful is it for customers to leave? (high: enterprise software, banks, telecom infrastructure; low: consumer apps, commodities)
- **Network Effects:** Does the product become more valuable as more people use it? (payments, marketplaces, social platforms) — most powerful moat type
- **Intangible Assets:** Patents, brand premium, regulatory licenses (pharma patents, utility franchises, airport landing slots)
- **Efficient Scale:** Serving a limited market so profitably that it deters new entrants (regional utilities, niche B2B software)

Moat erosion signals: gross margin declining >2pp/year for 3+ consecutive years, pricing power lost, customer win rates declining, new entrants taking share.

### Capital Allocation Quality (critical for long-term ratings)
Great operators allocate capital in this order of returns:
1. Reinvest in the business at high ROIC (best if ROIC > 15% and > WACC)
2. Strategic acquisitions at reasonable multiples (EV/EBITDA <12x for mature, <8x for declining)
3. Debt paydown when leverage is above target
4. Share buybacks when the stock trades below intrinsic value
5. Dividends (signals mature business, limits reinvestment flexibility)

Red flags in capital allocation:
- Acquisitions at >20x EBITDA in cyclical businesses
- Buybacks funded by new debt issuance
- Dividends maintained while FCF is declining (unsustainable payout)
- Goodwill exceeding 40% of total assets after serial acquisitions
- CEO compensation misaligned with shareholder returns (high pay during poor performance)

### DCF / Valuation Framework (qualitative, since market cap is unavailable)
Even without market price, assess the earnings power trajectory:
- **Earnings Power Value (EPV):** If growth stopped today, what would the steady-state FCF be? EPV = Normalized FCF / WACC. Compare to trends.
- **Trend Extrapolation:** At the current 3-yr revenue CAGR and current FCF margin, what does FCF look like in 3 years? Does it justify enthusiasm or skepticism?
- **Margin of Safety Assessment:** Companies with FCF margins >15%, declining debt, and consistent growth have wide margin of safety. Companies with thin FCF, rising debt, and decelerating growth have almost none.
- **EV/EBITDA relative to growth:** Fast-growing businesses (>20% revenue CAGR) can justify 20x+. Slow growers (<5% CAGR) should trade at <10x. Above those thresholds = priced for perfection.

### Earnings Quality Deep-Dive
A company can show GAAP profit while the underlying business is deteriorating. Check:
- **Accrual ratio** = (Net Income - OCF) / Average Total Assets. Above 5% = aggressive accruals, watch carefully.
- **Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) trend:** Rising DSO = customers taking longer to pay = revenue quality declining or channel stuffing.
- **Deferred revenue change:** Rising deferred revenue = customers paying in advance = strong demand signal. Falling = losing subscription/contract momentum.
- **Revenue recognition policy changes:** Any mention of ASC 606 adoption, contract modifications, or pull-forward recognition deserves scrutiny.
- **Non-GAAP adjustments exceeding 20% of GAAP earnings:** When "adjusted" EPS is routinely 30-50% above GAAP EPS, management is obscuring the true cost of the business.
- **OCF/Net Income ratio:** Consistently >1.0x = high quality. Below 0.7x for 2+ years = red flag. Below 0.5x = serious concern.

## Sector-Specific KPIs

### Technology / SaaS
- **ARR / MRR growth:** Annual/Monthly Recurring Revenue. Most important top-line metric. Deceleration from >50% to <20% is a warning.
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR) / Net Dollar Retention (NDR):** >120% = best-in-class (upsell exceeds churn); 100-110% = healthy; <100% = customers are shrinking or leaving.
- **CAC Payback Period:** <12 months = efficient; 12-24 months = acceptable; >24 months = expensive growth.
- **Rule of 40:** Revenue growth % + FCF margin % should equal ≥40 for a healthy SaaS business.
- **Gross margin:** >70% = SaaS; 50-70% = mixed software/services; <50% = heavily service-dependent, lower quality.
- **R&D as % of revenue:** 15-25% = investing for growth; >30% = heavy burn; <10% = milking existing product.

### Retail / Consumer
- **Same-Store Sales Growth (SSS/SSSG):** Organic comp growth excluding new store openings. Negative SSS is serious.
- **Inventory Turns:** Revenue / Inventory. Higher = better capital efficiency. Declining turns = product not moving.
- **Gross Margin %:** Key pricing power indicator. Compression means promotions eating into profitability.
- **SG&A as % of Revenue:** Should decline as revenue scales (operating leverage). Rising = cost control problem.
- **eCommerce penetration:** Growing or declining share of digital sales matters for long-term positioning.
- **Store productivity (Sales per sq ft):** Declining = real estate portfolio becoming a liability.

### Banks / Financial Services
- **Net Interest Margin (NIM):** Interest income minus interest expense / earning assets. >3% = strong; 2-3% = average; <2% = compressed.
- **Efficiency Ratio:** Non-interest expense / Revenue. <55% = efficient; 55-65% = average; >65% = bloated.
- **Non-Performing Loan (NPL) Ratio:** Non-performing loans / Total loans. <1% = strong; 1-2% = manageable; >3% = credit concern.
- **Tier 1 Capital Ratio:** >12% = well-capitalized; 10-12% = adequate; <10% = capital concern.
- **Return on Assets (ROA):** >1.5% = excellent; 1-1.5% = good; <0.5% = poor.
- **Loan-to-Deposit Ratio:** <80% = conservative; 80-90% = normal; >100% = aggressive funding.

### Oil & Gas / Energy
- **Production growth (BOE/day):** Barrels of oil equivalent per day — the top-line for upstream.
- **Reserve Replacement Ratio:** New reserves discovered / Production. >100% = replenishing; <100% = depleting asset base.
- **Finding & Development Cost (F&D):** Cost per new barrel added. Lower = better economics.
- **Lifting Cost / Production Cost (per BOE):** Operating cost per barrel. Must stay well below realized price.
- **EBITDAX margin:** EBITDA + Exploration expense — the cleaner profitability metric for E&P.
- **Debt/EBITDAX:** <2.0x = manageable; 2-3x = elevated for commodity-price-sensitive business; >3x = high risk.
- **Realized price vs. break-even:** Know the break-even oil/gas price for positive FCF. Companies with break-even >$60/bbl face existential risk in down cycles.

### Healthcare / Pharma / Biotech
- **Pipeline depth (pre-revenue biotech):** Number of Phase 2 / Phase 3 trials. Diversification reduces binary FDA risk.
- **Patent cliff exposure:** Revenue coming off patent protection within 3-5 years = serious headwind.
- **Gross margin:** >80% = strong pharma; 60-80% = medical devices; <50% = heavy manufacturing cost.
- **R&D as % of revenue:** 15-20% = responsible investment; >25% = burning for pipeline; <10% = depleting the portfolio.
- **FDA approval rate:** Historical success rate across submissions matters. Track record signals execution quality.
- **Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) with Medicare/Medicaid:** Government payers slow payment — rising DSO in healthcare is common but watch for unusual spikes.

### Industrials / Manufacturing
- **Book-to-Bill Ratio:** New orders / Revenue billed. >1.0 = backlog growing; <1.0 = demand softening.
- **Backlog coverage:** Total backlog / Annual revenue. >12 months = strong visibility; <6 months = limited forward visibility.
- **Capacity utilization:** Below 75% = meaningful fixed cost drag; above 90% = approaching constraints, may need CapEx.
- **CapEx intensity:** CapEx / Revenue. >10% = heavy capital requirements; <5% = asset-light. Maintenance vs. growth CapEx distinction matters.
- **Working capital efficiency:** Cash conversion cycle (DIO + DSO - DPO). Shorter cycle = better capital efficiency.
- **Segment mix shift:** Are higher-margin segments growing faster than lower-margin ones? This drives earnings quality.

## Other Stock Analysis Frameworks

### Technical Analysis Integration (context for timing, not fundamentals)
Use technicals to confirm or challenge fundamental conclusions, never as the primary driver:
- **Trend confirmation:** A strong fundamental story with price below all major moving averages (50/200-day) suggests either opportunity or unrecognized fundamental deterioration.
- **Volume signals:** High-volume breakouts confirm institutional conviction. High-volume breakdowns on bad news suggest forced selling, not panic.
- **Support / Resistance:** Former earnings support levels or 52-week lows provide reference points for discussing downside risk.
- **Relative strength:** If a stock is underperforming its sector peers on identical fundamentals, investigate why — something may be wrong not yet visible in filings.

### Factor Investing Lens (how institutional buyers categorize stocks)
- **Value factor:** Low P/E, low P/B, low EV/EBITDA relative to sector peers. Attractive to value investors when fundamentals are stable.
- **Quality factor:** High ROE (>20%), high gross margins, low debt, stable earnings. Quality stocks outperform over long periods. Look for these.
- **Momentum factor:** Recent price + earnings outperformance. Institutions follow momentum; a stock with worsening fundamentals but strong momentum is a timing problem.
- **Growth factor:** Revenue + EPS growth rate above 15% sustained. Commands premium multiples. Loses premium rapidly on deceleration.
- **Low volatility factor:** Defensive businesses with stable earnings trade at a premium during uncertainty. Utilities, consumer staples, healthcare.

### Event-Driven Analysis Triggers
Flag any of these events in the filing history or management tone analysis:
- **Spin-off or carve-out:** Often unlocks value when a subsidiary can be independently valued. New management focuses on one business.
- **Management change:** New CEO/CFO from operations background = usually constructive. From finance/legal background = potential restructuring or sale.
- **Activist investor entry:** Read their public letters. Activists identify specific operational improvements that create a catalyst.
- **Share repurchase authorization:** Only meaningful if the company has the FCF to execute it — check if buybacks are actually being completed.
- **Dividend initiation:** Signals management confidence in FCF durability. Dividend cuts are extremely negative catalysts.
- **Accounting restatement:** Treat as a serious red flag regardless of stated reason. Trust in management and financials is structurally damaged.
- **Going-concern language from auditor:** Existential warning. Immediate downgrade to Sell or Strong Sell unless a specific remedy is already in motion.

### ESG / Governance Risk Factors (from SEC filings)
Material governance risks to flag:
- **Dual-class share structure:** Founders retain control regardless of shareholder votes — limits accountability.
- **Audit committee red flags:** Frequent auditor changes, material weaknesses in internal controls, late 10-K filings.
- **Executive compensation misalignment:** LTIP targets that are easily achievable, options repriced after stock decline.
- **Board independence:** Boards dominated by insiders or founder allies reduce governance quality.
- **Environmental liability disclosure:** Growing legal reserves for EPA or environmental litigation = future cash outflow risk.
- **Cybersecurity risk disclosures:** Post-2023 SEC rules require material incident disclosure. Look for patterns of incidents.

## Multi-Year Trend Analysis Standards

### The 3-Year Rule
Never rate a company based on a single year's performance. The minimum trend window is:
- **3 years for a Buy** — needs consistent improvement across revenue, margins, and FCF.
- **2 years of deterioration = Hold** — one bad year can be noise, two is a trend.
- **3 years of deterioration = Sell** — at this point the business model is under structural pressure.

### Identifying False Recoveries
A company with 5 years of decline followed by 1 year of "recovery" is not a Buy. Require:
- At minimum 2 consecutive years of improvement before calling a recovery
- Improvement must be across multiple metrics (not just one line item)
- The improvement should match or exceed the pre-decline peak levels, not just improve from a low base
- MD&A language should reflect genuine operational confidence, not defensive optimism

### Peak-to-Current Analysis
For any metric, identify the historical peak and current level:
- Current gross margin vs. peak gross margin in the dataset = tells you how much value has been eroded
- Current FCF vs. peak FCF = tells you how far below peak cash generation the company is operating
- Current revenue growth vs. average historical growth = acceleration or deceleration story?

## Justification Paragraph Templates

### Revenue Paragraph (Para 1 blueprint)
"Revenue grew from [X] in FY[year] to [Y] in FY[year], representing a [Z]% 3-year CAGR. Growth [accelerated/decelerated] meaningfully in FY[year], with YoY growth [rate] compared to [prior rate] the year before. The [recent year] result [beat/trailed] the company's own guidance of [X]. [If negative: This deceleration is concerning because it coincides with X.] [If positive: The acceleration is supported by X.]"

### Margin Paragraph (Para 2 blueprint)
"Gross margin [expanded/compressed] from [X%] in FY[year] to [Y%] in FY[year]. [Operating/net] margin [trend description]. [If expanding: This reflects operating leverage — fixed costs spread over a larger revenue base.] [If compressing: This is the most important risk in this analysis. Margin compression over [N] consecutive years typically signals [pricing pressure/rising costs/mix shift toward lower-margin products].] FCF margin of [X%] in the latest year [supports/contradicts] the reported earnings picture."

### Risk Paragraph (Para 6 blueprint)
"The primary risks are: (1) [Specific risk] — [metric] [direction] from [X] to [Y] over FY[year]-FY[year], representing [magnitude] of deterioration. (2) [Second risk] — [specific data]. (3) [Third risk]. [If applicable: The convergence of [risk 1] and [risk 2] is particularly concerning because together they suggest [implication].]"

### What Would Change the Rating (Para 7 blueprint)
"A rating upgrade to [rating] would require: [specific measurable condition — e.g., 'two consecutive quarters of gross margin above 45% with YoY revenue growth resuming above 10%']. A downgrade to [rating] would follow: [specific deterioration — e.g., 'another FCF-negative year, or D/E ratio exceeding 2.5x without corresponding revenue acceleration']."
